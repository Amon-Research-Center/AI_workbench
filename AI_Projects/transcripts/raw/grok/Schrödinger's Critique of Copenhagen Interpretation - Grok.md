### **Preface (1944)**

A scientist is supposed to have a complete and thorough knowledge, at first hand, of some subjects and, therefore, is usually expected not to write on any topic of which he is not a master... Yet look at the fair thing Fate has done to me... I am no biologist... The large, important and very much discussed question is: How can the events in space and time which take place within the spatial boundary of a living organism be accounted for by physics and chemistry?

The preliminary answer which this little book will endeavor to expound and establish can be summarized as follows: The obvious inability of present-day physics and chemistry to account for such events is no reason at all for doubting that they can be accounted for by those sciences.

That would be taking too much for granted. Even if I am not a biologist, I at least have devoted much more thought and study to the biological question discussed here than to my own subject of physics.

We have inherited from our forefathers the keen longing for unified, all-embracing knowledge. The very name given to the highest institutions of learning reminds us, that from antiquity and throughout many centuries the universal aspect has been the only one to be given full credit. But the spread, both in width and depth, of the multifarious branches of knowledge during the last hundred odd years has confronted us with a queer dilemma... The reason is very simple: the subject-matter has become so vast that no individual mind can ever hope to command more than a small portion of it.

I can see no other escape from this dilemma (lest the scientist's object should forever remain a blur and a jumble) than that some of us should venture to embark on a synthesis of facts and theories, albeit with second-hand and incomplete knowledge of some of them - and at the risk of making fools of ourselves. ### **Chapter 1: The Classical Physicist's Approach to the Subject**

The physicist's most dreaded opponent is the biologist... This little book arose from a course of public lectures, delivered by a theoretical physicist to an audience of about four hundred which did not substantially dwindle, though warned at the outset that the subject-matter was a difficult one and that the lectures could not be termed popular, even though the physicist's most dreaded weapon, mathematical deduction, would hardly be utilized.

The reason for this was not that the subject was simple enough to be explained without mathematics, but rather that it was much too involved to be fully accessible to mathematics. In a way this was satisfying to me, because it showed the enormous difference (at least in my particular field) between the work of the theoretical physicist and that of the experimentalist.

Why are the atoms so small? The question is most natural, as you will presently see. For it is the very first question that you must ask if you want to understand the physicist's views about the organism.

From the standpoint of physics, we must assume that the structure in question is, by our standards, exceedingly complex. The arrangement of parts is exceedingly complicated; so much so that we cannot expect to be able to unravel it by applying the rather clumsy and limited means of observation which we have at our disposal at the present time.

The large and important and very much discussed question is: How can the events in space and time which take place within the spatial boundary of a living organism be accounted for by physics and chemistry? The preliminary answer is: The obvious inability of present-day physics and chemistry to account for such events is no reason at all for doubting that they can be accounted for by those sciences.

Statistical physics... The laws of physics are statistical laws. They have a lot to do with the natural tendency of things to go over into disorder. But, to reconcile the high durability of the hereditary substance with its minute size, we must evade the tendency to disorder by 'inventing the molecule', in fact, by invoking the principle of quantum theory.

(Continuing with discussions on atomic statistics, precision via large numbers, paramagnetism, Brownian motion, diffusion, measurement limits, and the  $\sqrt{n}$  rule for precision.)

### **Epilogue: On Determinism and Free Will**

As a reward for the serious trouble I have taken to expound the purely scientific aspects of our problem _sine ira et studio_, I beg leave to add my own, necessarily subjective, view of the philosophical implications.

According to the evidence put forward in the preceding pages the space-time events in the body of a living being which correspond to the activity of its mind, to its self-conscious or any other actions, are (considering also their complex structure and the accepted statistical explanation of physico-chemistry) if not strictly deterministic at any rate statistico-deterministic.

To the physicist I wish to emphasize that in my opinion, and contrary to the opinion upheld in some quarters, quantum indeterminacy plays no biologically relevant role in them, except perhaps by enhancing their purely accidental character in such events as meiosis, natural and X-ray-induced mutation and so on – and this is in any case obvious and well recognized.

For the biologist I am tempted to put it as follows: Life would be a phenomenon entirely different from all others in Nature, if the expectant mother consciously and purposefully directed the share which each of the little cells is to take in the growth of the embryo.
