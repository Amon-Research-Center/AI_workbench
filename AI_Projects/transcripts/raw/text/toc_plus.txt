✅ Exporting from Jupyter Notebook (JupyterLab or Classic)
1. Export as .ipynb (Native)
    • This is the default format—JSON under the hood.
    • You can always convert .ipynb to .json (they're functionally the same).
    • Use:
        ◦ File → Download As → Notebook (.ipynb)
        ◦ Or manually rename .ipynb to .json for version control use.
2. Export as .py (Scripted Markdown/Code)
    • Use jupyter nbconvert:
      jupyter nbconvert --to script my_notebook.ipynb
    • Produces a readable .py file with Markdown in comments.
3. Export as .yaml
    • Not supported natively by Jupyter, but:
        ◦ You can write a simple exporter with nbformat:
          import nbformat
          import yaml

          with open('my_notebook.ipynb') as f:
              nb = nbformat.read(f, as_version=4)

          with open('my_notebook.yaml', 'w') as out:
              yaml.dump(nb, out)
        ◦ This outputs a YAML version of the notebook structure (cells, metadata, outputs, etc.)

Chapter: Mathematical Foundations of Field-Based Models
Overview
This chapter establishes the formal mathematical scaffolding necessary to understand the theory and dynamics introduced in this dissertation. It acts as both primer and springboard — introducing the algebraic, geometric, and categorical tools required to comprehend the Base Morphogenetic Field (BMF) and its symbolic structure.
It begins by reviewing the core principles of set theory, algebraic operations, tensor structures, and category theory. The chapter then transitions into foundational field theory, leading up to the generative framework of operator-based transformation used throughout the dissertation. Particular emphasis is placed on the tension between continuity and discreteness — and the need for symbolic representation when modeling phenomena at or beyond physical resolution.

I. From Sets to Structures
    • Set-theoretic foundation: $\mathcal{U}$ as a universal space
    • Elements as entities of interaction: $a \in A \subset \mathcal{U}$
    • Mapping and relations: functions $f: A \to B$ and morphisms
Motivation: Systems are not objects, but relations between stable domains of potential.

II. Algebraic Systems
    • Groups, rings, fields: structure-preserving operations
    • Operator definitions: closure, associativity, identity, invertibility
    • Modular algebras and multi-valued logic
    • Commutative vs non-commutative systems
Application: Operators in BMF theory must preserve structure and enable transformation across symbolic topologies.

III. Tensor Spaces and Multilinear Maps
    • Vectors as morphic primitives
    • Tensors as multi-dimensional relations
    • Inner product and metric construction
    • Tensor contraction and flow representation
Bridge: Tensor structure enables field expressions that map local variation to global coherence.

IV. Foundations of Category Theory
    • Categories, objects, and arrows
    • Functors as structure-preserving mappings
    • Natural transformations
    • Commutative diagrams as expressive syntax
Justification: BMF models depend on symbolic operators acting functorially across model layers.

V. Scalar and Vector Fields
    • Scalar fields: $\phi(x, y, t)$ over space-time
    • Vector fields: $\vec{F}(x, y, z)$ as direction + magnitude
    • Gradient ($\nabla$), divergence ($\nabla \cdot$), and curl ($\nabla \times$)
    • Partial derivatives and multi-scale resolution
Example: The base point operator $P(x, y, t)$ evolves into higher-order constructs via derivative stacking.

VI. Symbolic Formulation and Discrete Evolution
    • Operators as symbolic transformers
