SECTION 1: A MODEL OF THE EMBRYO. MORPHOGENS

Sentence 1:
"In this section a mathematical model of the growing embryo will be described."
Expansion:
Turing signals: "I'm building a simplified model of how organisms develop form." Think of it as a blueprint of form-making, not life itself.
Tool: Conceptual framework — no formula yet.

Sentence 2:
"This model will be a simplification and an idealization, and consequently a falsification."
Expansion:
He admits it’s an abstraction — not a perfect mirror of biology but a reduction to highlight generative essentials.
Tool: Model theory — simplification engine.

Sentence 3:
"It is to be hoped that the features retained for discussion are those of greatest importance in the present state of knowledge."
Expansion:
This is triage: he keeps what matters most for today's understanding and leaves out complexity beyond reach.
Tool: Feature selection (conceptual).

Sentence 4:
"The model takes two slightly different forms."
Expansion:
Fork in the path. One uses discrete cells, the other smooth fields.

Sentence 5:
"In one of them the cell theory is recognized but the cells are idealized into geometrical points."
Expansion:
Cells = abstract dots. Think of a lattice or particle simulation. Tool: Lattice model / discrete grid
Display: 2D/3D matrix of points with values at nodes.

Sentence 6:
"In the other the matter of the organism is imagined as continuously distributed."
Expansion:
Smooth field. Think of a fabric with gradients of chemical properties. Tool: Scalar/vector field (continuum mechanics)
Display: 2D heatmap / 3D volume field (isosurfaces, gradients).

Sentence 7:
"The cells are not, however, completely ignored, for various physical and physico-chemical characteristics of the matter as a whole are assumed to have values appropriate to the cellular matter."
Expansion:
Even in the smooth model, assumptions are based on cell-like properties: elasticity, resistance, etc. It’s a hybrid model.
Tool: Parameter embedding (biological realism in physics).

Sentence 8:
"With either of the models one proceeds as with a physical theory and defines an entity called the state of the system."
Expansion:
The "state" is the full snapshot of position, velocity, concentration, etc. at a moment in time. Tool: State vector (dynamical systems)
Used in ODE/PDE solvers and simulations.

Sentence 9:
"One then describes how that state is to be determined from the state at a moment very shortly before."
Expansion:
This is motion through time: differential equations update the system forward in tiny steps. Tool: Time evolution equation
Example: Euler or Runge-Kutta method to integrate ∂x/∂t.

Sentence 10:
"With either model the description of the state consists of two parts, the mechanical and the chemical."
Expansion:
Two layers: physical forces (movement, tension) and chemical (concentration, diffusion, reaction). Tool: Coupled systems (mechanics + chemistry)
Display: dual-layer sim — one for motion, one for chemical field.

Sentence 11:
"The mechanical part of the state describes the positions, masses, velocities and elastic properties of the cells, and the forces between them."
Expansion:
Physics layer — it's a soft-body simulation of elastic blobs. Tool: Elastic solid mechanics
Equations: Hooke's law, Navier-Cauchy equations
Display: mesh deformation or spring-mass system.

Sentence 12:
"In the continuous form of the theory essentially the same information is given in the form of the stress, velocity, density and elasticity of the matter."
Expansion:
In the smooth case, this becomes a vector/tensor field.
Tool: Stress-energy tensor field (continuum mechanics)
Display: flow field + vector arrows over surface.

Sentence 13:
"The chemical part of the state is given (in the cell form of theory) as the chemical composition of each separate cell; the diffusibility of each substance between each two adjacent cells must also be given."
Expansion:
Each node has concentrations; edges between them have diffusion constants.
Tool: Discrete reaction-diffusion network
Equations: C_i(t+1) = C_i(t) + D(C_j - C_i) + R(C_i)
Display: 2D grid color map or graph edges with flow.

Sentence 14:
"In the continuous form of the theory the concentrations and diffusibilities of each substance have to be given at each point."
Expansion:
This gives rise to the famous reaction-diffusion PDE:
Tool: Reaction-Diffusion Equation
Equation: ∂C/∂t = D∇²C + R(C)
Display: heatmaps, isosurfaces, evolving textures in 2D/3D.

Sentence 15:
"In determining the changes of state one should take into account:"
Expansion:
Turing is about to give us a checklist—the driving laws for system evolution. Tool: State transition framework — input into simulator logic.

Sentence 16 (i):
"The changes of position and velocity as given by Newton’s laws of motion."
Expansion:
Standard physics—forces cause acceleration, etc. Tool: Newtonian mechanics
Equation: F = ma, integrated over time
Display: vector motion, elastic mesh dynamics.

Sentence 17 (ii):
"The stresses as given by the elasticities and motions, also taking into account the osmotic pressures as given from the chemical data."
Expansion:
Physical tension between particles AND pressure from chemical gradients. Tool: Elastic stress + Osmotic pressure modeling
Equation: σ = Eε (stress = elasticity × strain), plus osmotic ∇Π
Display: deformation plus color-encoded pressure maps.

Sentence 18 (iii):
"The chemical reactions."
Expansion:
Reactions change concentrations locally based on current values. Tool: Reaction kinetics
Equation: dC/dt = k[A][B]...
Display: intensity shifts over time (heatmaps).

Sentence 19 (iv):

Diffusion is shaped by the tissue’s structure—boundaries and geometry. Tool: Coupled reaction-diffusion + mechanical geometry constraints
Equation: ∂C/∂t = D∇²C within available space
Display: diffusing blobs respecting geometry.
